GDPR Website Compliance for UK Businesses: A Complete Guide (2026)

By TheoMedia Editorial Team 2 June 2026 11 min read Business

The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) issued fines totalling over £50 million between 2018 and 2025 for GDPR breaches — and that's just the cases that became public. Thousands of smaller businesses have received warnings, enforcement notices, and smaller fines that never make the news. The most common source of violations isn't a catastrophic data breach — it's a poorly configured website that collects data without proper consent, uses non-compliant cookie banners, or lacks a legally adequate privacy policy.

This guide explains exactly what UK GDPR requires from your website in 2026, the practical steps to achieve compliance, and the most common mistakes that leave businesses exposed. Note: this article is informational guidance — for specific legal advice, consult a qualified data protection solicitor or the ICO directly.

What Is GDPR and Does It Still Apply After Brexit?

The EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) came into force on 25 May 2018 and fundamentally changed data privacy law across Europe. When the UK left the EU, GDPR was incorporated into UK domestic law as the UK GDPR, which sits alongside the Data Protection Act 2018. The UK GDPR is enforced by the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO).

In practice, UK GDPR and EU GDPR are very similar — the principles, rights, obligations, and penalty structures are largely aligned. The key differences are in international data transfers (where the UK has its own adequacy framework) and certain administrative provisions. For most UK small and medium businesses, the practical compliance requirements are identical.

If your website serves users in EU member states (which it likely does if you sell or market to European customers), you must comply with both UK GDPR and EU GDPR — though in practice, complying with either typically satisfies both.

What a GDPR-Compliant Website Must Have

1. A Compliant Cookie Consent Banner

Cookie consent is where most websites fail. The ICO has been very clear: pre-ticked consent boxes are not valid consent. A cookie banner that says "By continuing to use this website, you consent to cookies" is not compliant. Valid cookie consent must be:

  • Freely given: Users must be able to decline non-essential cookies without any disadvantage — the "X" button and "Reject All" option must be as prominent as "Accept All".
  • Specific: Users must be able to consent to different categories of cookies separately (analytics, marketing, preferences).
  • Informed: The banner must clearly explain what each category of cookie does.
  • Unambiguous: Consent must be a clear affirmative action — scrolling or continued browsing doesn't count.
  • Revocable: Users must be able to withdraw consent as easily as they gave it, typically via a "Cookie Settings" link in the footer.

Non-essential cookies — including Google Analytics — must not fire until the user has actively accepted them. This is a common implementation error; many developers fire analytics scripts immediately and display the consent banner afterwards. Under UK GDPR, analytics cookies require consent before they run.

2. A Legally Adequate Privacy Policy

Every website that processes personal data must have a privacy policy that's easy to find, easy to read, and legally complete. It must cover:

  • What personal data you collect and how (contact forms, analytics, cookies, email signups)
  • The legal basis for processing each type of data (consent, legitimate interest, contract, legal obligation)
  • How long you retain each type of data
  • Who you share data with (third-party processors, analytics platforms, payment processors)
  • Users' rights under UK GDPR (access, rectification, erasure, portability, objection)
  • How to contact you for data requests (a dedicated email address or form)
  • Your ICO registration number if you're required to be registered
  • Details of any international data transfers

3. Compliant Contact Forms

A simple contact form that collects a name, email, and message is processing personal data. To comply with UK GDPR:

  • Include a link to your privacy policy on or near the form with a statement like "Your data will be processed in accordance with our Privacy Policy".
  • Do not include a pre-ticked consent checkbox — if you're relying on consent as your legal basis, the checkbox must be unticked and the user must actively tick it.
  • Only collect the data you actually need — don't add fields "just in case".
  • Store form submissions securely and define a retention period (e.g., "We retain contact enquiry data for 12 months then delete it").

Common mistake: Many contact form plugins store all form submissions in the WordPress database indefinitely. This means you may be holding personal data for years without a clear retention policy — a GDPR violation. Check your form plugin settings and configure automatic deletion after your defined retention period.

4. SSL / HTTPS

Transmitting personal data over an unencrypted HTTP connection is a failure of the GDPR principle of "appropriate technical security measures." All websites that collect personal data (i.e., all websites with contact forms, login areas, or ecommerce) must use HTTPS. This is now industry standard and free via Let's Encrypt — there's no excuse for an HTTP-only website in 2026.

5. Data Processing Agreements with Third Parties

If your website uses third-party services that process your users' personal data on your behalf — Google Analytics, Mailchimp, HubSpot, Stripe, Hotjar — you need Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) in place with each of them. Most major platforms provide standard DPAs automatically or on request. Review each tool you use and confirm DPAs are in place.

Common GDPR Mistakes on UK Business Websites

  1. Firing Google Analytics before consent: The most common mistake. Analytics must wait for consent approval — not the other way around.
  2. No "Reject All" option on cookie banners: A consent mechanism that only offers "Accept" and "more information" is not compliant — users must be able to decline all non-essential cookies in one click.
  3. Generic, copy-pasted privacy policies: A privacy policy that doesn't accurately reflect the actual tools and data you collect is potentially worse than no policy — it can be evidence of deliberate misrepresentation.
  4. No retention policy: GDPR requires you to define how long you keep data. "We keep it for as long as necessary" is not sufficient — you need specific timeframes.
  5. Embedding YouTube videos without consent: Embedded YouTube videos set tracking cookies. Under GDPR, these require consent. Use YouTube's "privacy-enhanced mode" (nocookie.com URL) or a facade that loads the embed only after user interaction.
  6. Google Fonts loaded from Google's servers: Loading Google Fonts from fonts.googleapis.com sends users' IP addresses to Google without their consent. Self-host your fonts instead — it's also faster.
  7. Contact forms emailing data to an unsecured inbox: If contact form data is emailed to a Gmail or unencrypted inbox, ensure it's accessed only from secure devices and deleted when no longer needed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does GDPR still apply to UK businesses after Brexit?

Yes. GDPR principles are retained in UK domestic law as UK GDPR, enforced by the ICO. The requirements are very similar to EU GDPR. If your website also serves EU users, you may need to comply with both — though a website built to UK GDPR standards will typically satisfy EU GDPR requirements as well.

What cookies require consent under UK GDPR?

Non-essential cookies require explicit, opt-in consent before being set. This includes analytics cookies (including Google Analytics), advertising and tracking cookies, and social media embed cookies. Strictly necessary cookies — such as session cookies required for the website to function — do not require consent but must be disclosed in your privacy policy.

Does my small business website need a privacy policy?

Yes. Any website that collects personal data — which includes contact forms, email newsletter signups, and analytics — must have a privacy policy. It must explain what data you collect, why you collect it, how long you keep it, and how users can request deletion or access. Failing to have one is a breach of UK GDPR.

What is a Data Processing Agreement and do I need one?

A DPA is a contract between you (data controller) and any third party that processes personal data on your behalf (data processor). If you use Google Analytics, Mailchimp, HubSpot, Stripe, or similar tools on your website, you need DPAs with each. Most major providers offer standard DPAs — check your account settings or contact their support teams.

What are the penalties for GDPR non-compliance in the UK?

The ICO can issue fines of up to £17.5 million or 4% of global annual turnover for serious breaches. For less severe violations, fines can reach £8.7 million or 2% of global turnover. Beyond fines, the ICO can issue enforcement notices and require you to stop processing data — which could effectively shut down parts of your website's functionality.
